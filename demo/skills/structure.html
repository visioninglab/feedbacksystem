What is essay structure?

Essay structure is the way you organise your ideas into a coherent whole. A well-structured essay has a clear introduction that frames the question, body paragraphs that each make a distinct point, and a conclusion that synthesises the argument. Good structure acts as a roadmap ‚Äî it tells the reader where you are going and why. Without it, even strong ideas can feel disconnected and hard to follow.

üìñ Example 1: Well-Structured Introduction
Student Writing
Attachment theory, first proposed by Bowlby (1969), argues that the bond formed between an infant and their primary caregiver has lasting effects on emotional development. This essay will evaluate the extent to which early attachment shapes adult relationships by examining evidence from Ainsworth's Strange Situation paradigm, longitudinal studies of attachment stability, and cross-cultural research that challenges the universality of Bowlby's claims. It will argue that while early attachment is influential, its effects are moderated by subsequent experiences and cultural context.
Why this works: The introduction does three things clearly: (1) it defines the key concept ‚Äî attachment theory ‚Äî with a citation, (2) it tells the reader exactly what the essay will cover, and (3) it states the argument the essay will make. The reader finishes this paragraph knowing what to expect from the rest of the essay.
üìñ Example 2: Weak Conclusion vs. Improved Conclusion
‚ùå Weak Version
In conclusion, attachment theory is very important and has been studied by many psychologists. There are many different views on this topic.
Why this is weak: This conclusion is vague and could apply to almost any psychology essay. It doesn't summarise the specific evidence discussed, doesn't answer the essay question, and doesn't reach a considered position.
‚úÖ Improved Version
The evidence examined in this essay suggests that early attachment has a significant but not deterministic influence on emotional development. While Bowlby's theory and supporting research from Hazan and Shaver (1987) demonstrate that attachment styles formed in infancy can persist into adulthood, Rutter's longitudinal work with Romanian orphans shows that recovery is possible when children are placed in supportive environments. Future research should examine the specific mechanisms through which later experiences modify early attachment patterns.
Why this is better: This conclusion synthesises the key evidence, states a clear position that answers the question, acknowledges nuance, and suggests a direction for further research. It feels like the essay has reached a considered endpoint.
